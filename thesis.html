THESIS RESEARCH — NATURAL COASTAL INFRASTRUCTURE

Quantifying the Protective Value of Blue-Green Infrastructure in Mitigating Coastal Disasters

Almost two-thirds of the world’s most populous cities are located in areas vulnerable to sea-level rise. Even if collective human intervention limits increases in global temperature to 2°C in the next 30 years, 570 cities around the world will still be affected by coastal inundation. While managed retreat may be the only option for cities particularly at-risk, others must proactively implement solutions to adapt to these predicted circumstances, which are quickly becoming a reality today. Known as blue-green infrastructure (BGI), the restoration and integration of natural landscapes within the urban fabric is emerging as an effective alternative to, and in some cases, co-intervention with grey infrastructure like human-made sea-walls and breakwaters.

Using Metro Vancouver as a case study, this research is the first in the region to quantify the protective role of coastal natural infrastructure during disaster events. Though BGI has been implemented in a few low-wave energy sites in the Metro Vancouver area, namely along existing public beaches, British Columbia’s regulatory framework does not currently support blue-green infrastructure or soft engineering approaches for disaster mitigation. As the city shifts into the second phase of its sea-level rise strategy, quantifying the value of BGI in physical, social and economic terms could aid future mitigation efforts and help better communicate its benefits to stakeholders beyond the scientific realm.

DISASTER PLANNING — SALVADOR DE BAHIA

In 2017, Salvador became the second most unequal city in Brazil in terms of income distribution. Due to the socio-economic challenges they face, many of the city's residents have no choice but to settle illegally, causing unplanned and rapid urban expansion that destabilizes the land and degrades the environment. As such, marginalized populations are disproportionately affected by disaster events compared to their counterparts in higher socio-economic strata.

As part of a remote, international team, myself and 5 MBA students worked with Salvador's government and community representatives to assess and develop proposals to address the city’s 100RC Resilience Challenge:

“The City of Salvador, Brazil experiences regular heavy rainfall that can lead to devastating urban landslides. In the last 10 years, the Municipal Civil Defense and other government agencies have assisted in over 19,000 landslide related occurrences, providing support to rebuild property and in many cases relocate citizens. Further engineering work is underway with federal support to stabilize landslide-prone neighborhoods, but rapid population growth increases the possibility of future risks."


“We’re not trying to be rich, just trying to live better”
Martha Schoeler, Lead Resilience Strategist for the City of Salvador

Our team proposed four complementary approaches to address the physical, socio-economic, and environmental impacts of landslides and the risks these events pose to human life and property. In lieu of relocation efforts, the proposals put forward by our team recognize informal settlements as legitimate human communities, focusing instead on strengthening neighbourhood cohesion, leveraging green infrastructure to stablize risk areas, utilizing technology for pro-active disaster response, and increasing the capacity of residents to drive community-led and informed initiatives forward.

  • Agroforestry & hillslope stabilization
  • Conditional cash transfer program
  • Safety training and mobilization app
  • Leadership development and increased community autonomy
